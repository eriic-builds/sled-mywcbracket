sled-mywcbracket — the build story

How the zero-backend social layer got planned, built, verified, and shipped — first as a pilot (5 stages), then promoted to the main repo and hardened in Round 2 (rename, navigation, and the actual-path bracket rebuild). What I did, why, what went wrong, and what each bump teaches. Built July 7–8, 2026, mid-tournament.

The problem, in one sentence

Let coworkers compare World Cup brackets for $0 and without putting anyone's name or picks anywhere they didn't personally opt into.

Your insight became the architecture

You said: "could I do like a share link that adds that user into your dashboard, so we're not compromising everyone who doesn't want it to be surfaceable?" — that IS the design. No backend was needed because:

A bracket is only 31 yes/no choices. Each match has two possible winners, and there are 31 matches (16+8+4+2+1). That's 31 bits ≈ 4 bytes of real information. Add a name and a tiebreaker and the whole thing fits in a ~140-character URL. Something that small doesn't need a database — the link itself is the database.
🧒 ELI5: Your bracket is like 31 coin flips written down. That's so little information it fits on the address line of an envelope — you don't need to rent a warehouse to store it.

The social loop (what shipped)

1 · Sharetap 🔗 Share link, optionally change the name ("share as E.L.")
▼ send the link to whoever YOU choose (chat, email)
2 · Viewthey see your full live-scored dashboard, view-only — their own bracket untouched
▼ one tap
3 · ➕ Add to my leaderboardyour bracket lands on THEIR device only
▼
4 · 🏆 Leaderboardlive ranking + "where we differ" for the games still to play

There is no step 5. No server, no pool code, no registry, no bill, nothing to deprecate.

Why first: we were about to modify the glue code hard, mid-tournament. The golden snapshot (render output frozen against frozen inputs) + CI meant every later stage had a tripwire. 🧒 ELI5: Before renovating, we installed smoke detectors (tests that run on every change) and photocopied the blueprints (SPEC.md) — in a practice house, so the real house stays safe.
1

Share links — the bracket-in-a-URL

commit 97e3c67 · new share.js (pure encode/decode), view-only mode, "share as" alias, 10-check test

What: encode = walk the 31 matches in canonical order, write "0" or "1" per pick, wrap with name + tiebreaker in JSON, base64url it. Decode = replay the bits in the same order (later rounds depend on earlier picks, so order is load-bearing). Result: a 138-character URL carrying a whole bracket.

The two data-loss traps dodged: (a) opening someone's link must never overwrite your saved bracket — the shared view renders without persisting; (b) previews used to wipe your what-if edits (a pre-existing bug: viewing the demo destroyed them) — fixed with a stash/restore in storage.js.

Bump 3: First E2E screenshot showed "✓ saved on this device" on a shared bracket — untrue and creepy. Caught only because the flow was walked in a real browser, not just unit-tested. Lesson: unit tests prove the math; only using the thing catches the lies in the UI.
Bump 4: A broken link's error message was being instantly covered by the auto-opening saved bracket — silent failure. Now a bad link keeps you on the landing with a plain-words explanation ("ask the sender to copy it again"). Lesson (Hanselman): every error must say what broke and what to do.
🧒 ELI5: We taught the site to write your bracket as a secret code on the envelope, and taught it to read such envelopes from friends — while promising to never scribble over YOUR homework when you look at theirs.
2+3

Add-to-leaderboard + the leaderboard itself

commit 22d4bb5 · new compare.js: rivals store, standings, diff, rename/remove; 12-check test

The one-button social loop: viewing a shared bracket shows ➕ Add to my leaderboard; tapping it stores the bracket locally and drops you straight into the ranked standings — the payoff moment. Rivals are deduped by a content hash (not by name — two Alexes are legal), capped at 20.

The number that can't lie: the leaderboard's "confirmed" column is computed by the same computeState() the dashboard uses, and a test asserts the leaderboard number equals the number rendered on the dashboard for the same bracket. The two can never drift apart.

The diff that matters: "where we differ" lists only undecided games (decided ones are already in the score), sorted by points at stake — so the top line is always the biggest remaining swing. Compared by bracket slot, not by team: two brackets can both "have Spain in the semis" via different paths.

Bump 5: Verified in-browser: rename to "Dave from Sales" persisted; pasting your own link answers "That's your own bracket 🙂". The "diff" button also rendered when you had no bracket of your own to diff against — clicking did nothing. Now it's hidden until your bracket exists. Lesson: every button visible on screen is a promise; don't show promises you can't keep.
🧒 ELI5: Friends' guess-sheets go on your fridge (and only your fridge). The fridge sorts them by score, and highlights exactly which upcoming games could shuffle the order.
4

Polish: make the payoff visible + honest words

commit 65d4019 · gold Leaderboard button (first in the bar, with count), landing "Your pool" card, demo affordance, privacy copy, README

Why: the leaderboard is the whole point of the pilot, but it was one grey button among five. Now it's gold, first, and shows the count — "🏆 Leaderboard (2)". A returning visitor with rivals sees a "Your pool — N saved" card right on the landing. A solo visitor can add the demo bracket to feel the leaderboard before any friend sends a link.

Bump 6 (deployment): The Pages deploys for Stages 0–1 failed: actions/configure-pages can't create the Pages site on a brand-new repo — the workflow token isn't an admin ("Resource not accessible by integration"). On the original repo this never showed because Pages had been enabled by hand once. Fix: one API call (gh api POST /repos/…/pages -f build_type=workflow), then re-run — green ever since. Lesson: "works in repo A" can hide one-time manual setup someone did long ago; new environments flush those assumptions out.
Bump 7 (verification craft): Twice, a screenshot showed a stale mid-load frame that contradicted reality (viewer bar "visible" on the landing). Reading the actual DOM state (element.hidden, localStorage) settled it each time. And one "Save as mine" click landed on stale coordinates — a direct DOM click proved the handler was fine. Lesson (Willison): when evidence conflicts, prefer the ground truth (DOM/state) over the rendering of it (pixels).
🧒 ELI5: We put the trophy button in gold at the front, added a "your fridge already has 2 sheets" reminder at the door, and rewrote the fine print so a non-lawyer can read it.

Round 2 — pilot promoted to main (July 8)

You test-drove the pilot, liked it, and reported four things. Here's what each one was, what the real cause turned out to be, and what got shipped.

①

Rename: wc26-pool-pilot → sled-mywcbracket

one gh repo rename + a sweep of every URL in the repo

GitHub migrated the Pages site to the new address automatically and redirects the old repo URLs. Every reference — README badges, <link rel="canonical">, og:tags, the deploy workflow's environment URL, CLAUDE.md, this file — was swept in one pass, then the "Pilot" framing was retired: this is now the main social edition.

Gotcha for later: any share links or bookmarks made under the OLD Pages URL (…/wc26-pool-pilot/) do not redirect — GitHub redirects repo pages, not github.io sites. Only test links existed, so nothing was lost — but it's why renames should happen early.
🧒 ELI5: We changed the restaurant's name before printing the real menus. The post office forwards mail to the new name, but the old street sign is just gone — good thing only friends had the old address.
②③

Navigation: trapped in the demo + "New bracket" → "🏠 Home"

files: main.js, index.html

Your bug report was two symptoms of one design gap: every "go back" button was secretly destructive. "New bracket" and "Clear" both wiped your saved bracket — and the demo view showed no buttons at all, because all the owner buttons hide in demo mode and nothing non-destructive existed to show. So: no way out of the demo, and a scary mislabeled "New bracket".

The fix is a separation of concerns: 🏠 Home goes to the start page and never deletes anything (it also restores stashed what-if edits and cleans the URL) — so it's safe to show in every mode: own, demo, and shared. Clear remains the one destructive button, and says so. And since the landing can now be reached while a bracket is saved, it gained an "📊 Open my dashboard — Eric's bracket is saved here" card, closing the loop.

🧒 ELI5: Before, the only doors out of any room were marked "burn the house down." Now there's a normal front door (Home) in every room, and the matches are in a clearly-labeled drawer (Clear).
④

The big one: the actual-path bracket rebuild

files: render.js (the byte-locked engine — intentionally), interact.js, dashboard.css + new tests/bracketmap.mjs

Reproduce before you fix: I generated a worst-case bracket (every pick a loser) and rendered it headlessly. Result: the "Actual path" view showed 8 dead-blank cells — fine through the QF column, then nothing: 4 blank semifinal cells, 2 blank final cells, blank champion. And since blank boxes carry no team identity, connector lines had nothing to attach to — your "lines don't connect" symptom was the same bug wearing a different hat.

Root cause: the old code derived "who actually advanced" by walking your pick tree (asking "what happened in the match where my pick played?"). When your picks bust out early, that chain of questions dead-ends — even though the real tournament data knows exactly who's in each slot.

The rebuild: each bracket slot now reads its occupant directly from real results — "who won this slot's feeder match?" — completely independent of your picks. Decided slot + not your pick → blue ▲ box. Undecided slot + your pick busted → a dashed "Winner M97" placeholder instead of a void, so the path structure stays visible (and column heights stable) all the way to the champion. Your-pick-alive cells are untouched, and the "My picks" view wasn't touched at all — you said it was accurate, and the tests confirm it.

Connectors got a second fix too: a ResizeObserver on the bracket plus a redraw when webfonts finish loading. Lines used to be drawn once at load; any layout shift afterward (font swap, collapsing/expanding a section, theme change) left them pointing at stale coordinates.

Testing surprise worth learning from: after the rebuild, the golden snapshot… passed unchanged. Why? The demo bracket is a good bracket — its actual view never had a blank cell, so the snapshot never exercised the broken path. The fixture only protects what its data reaches. That's why a dedicated tests/bracketmap.mjs now locks the worst case: all-losers bracket → zero blanks, every decided slot shows its real occupant, every undecided busted slot shows "Winner MXX", and an all-correct bracket shows no ▲ at all.
🧒 ELI5 (the bug): The old map drew the river by following YOUR paper boat. When your boat sank at the first waterfall, the map just… stopped drawing the river. The new map draws the river from the river itself — your boat is a sticker on top.
🧒 ELI5 (the testing surprise): A smoke detector in the kitchen can't notice a fire in the garage. The golden test watched a good bracket; the bug only lived in terrible brackets — so we installed a detector in the garage too.
⑤

Bonus bump: the robot pushed while we worked

git push rejected mid-round

The results-sync bot committed fresh World Cup results to the repo while this round was in progress (it's QF week — that's its job). A quick git pull --rebase, all 7 test suites re-run green on top of the new results, push. 🧒 ELI5: The mailman delivered while we were painting the hallway. Because the "before" photos we compare against are kept in an album (frozen fixtures), new mail on the table doesn't make the photos wrong.

Your question: "can I kill a share link later?"

The honest answer: no — and no design could truly give you that. A share link IS the data (the 31 picks + name travel inside it), like an email attachment or a photocopy. There's no server that recipients "check back with," so there's nothing to revoke against. And even a server-based revocation can't reach the copies people already saved, or a screenshot of the leaderboard.

What you CAN do, today:

  • Control the blast radius up front — the "share as" field exists exactly for this: share as "E.L." or an alias, and the link never contained your full name to begin with. That's prevention, which beats revocation.
  • Know what's actually at risk — a link contains picks + the display name you typed. Nothing else. Worst case of a leaked link: someone sees World Cup guesses.
  • Ask recipients to remove you — one ✕ on their leaderboard; brackets live only on devices, so removal there is complete.
  • Future option, recorded in SPEC.md — if real revocation ever matters, that's the one feature that justifies a tiny backend (links become references to stored brackets, and deleting the stored bracket kills every link). Deferred deliberately, not forgotten.
🧒 ELI5: Once you hand someone a photocopy, no button un-photocopies it — that's true for every app, even ones with servers (screenshots exist!). So instead of a magic eraser, we gave you a pen name: don't write your full name on the copy in the first place.

The two design rules that shaped every line

1 · New code goes around the byte-locked engine, not through it. render.js output is snapshot-frozen; the share popover, viewer-bar buttons, and overlay all live in index.html/main.js/new modules. Result: the golden test passed unchanged through all five stages — proof the dashboard every visitor sees is bit-for-bit what it was before the pilot.
2 · Pure core, thin shell. Everything testable is a pure function (encodeShare, decodeShare, standings, diffPicks); the DOM code is a thin shell around it. That's why 22 new unit checks could cover the real logic while the browser pass only had to verify wiring.
🧒 ELI5: The math lives in little calculator boxes you can test on a bench. The buttons and screens are just wires to the boxes. Test the boxes hard, then just check the wires are plugged in.

Learning moments 🎓

Seven transferable ideas, each with what happened here, the general principle, and the ELI5.

1 · Count the information before you build the infrastructure

Here: "social platform" sounded like servers, accounts, databases. Counting first — 31 binary picks ≈ 4 bytes — collapsed the whole backend question. The feature shipped as a URL format.

Principle: the size of the data, not the size of the idea, determines the architecture. Measure it before designing anything.

🧒 You don't rent a truck to move a house key. Weigh the cargo first — sometimes it fits in your pocket.
2 · The URL fragment is a privacy feature, not a hack

Here: the bracket travels after the # in the URL. By HTTP's own rules, browsers never send the fragment to the server — so picks and names stay out of every server log on earth, including GitHub's.

Principle: the strongest privacy guarantee is structural ("the data cannot arrive there") rather than promissory ("we won't look at it").

🧒 It's a note stapled to the inside of your jacket, not written on the envelope. The post office never reads it because it never gets it.
3 · Previews must never eat real data (the stash pattern)

Here: "view the demo" and "open a shared link" both re-render the dashboard — and the old code's cleanup logic treated that like a real bracket swap, deleting your what-if edits. Fix: stash the real state before a preview, restore when your bracket returns, and only stash-if-absent so two previews in a row can't overwrite the stash with preview junk.

Principle: when destructive cleanup is triggered by "state changed," enumerate every path that changes state. Demos, previews, and just-looking flows are the classic false positives.

🧒 The robot vacuum shouldn't eat your LEGO castle just because you stepped out to look at a friend's castle. Put yours on the shelf first, take it down when you're back.
4 · Two numbers that must agree should come from one function

Here: the leaderboard's "confirmed" and the dashboard's "confirmed" both come from the same computeState(), and a test asserts they're equal for the same bracket. There is no way for the leaderboard to say 23 while the dashboard says 25.

Principle: never re-derive the same fact in two places. If you must, chain them with a test so drift is impossible.

🧒 One referee, shown on two scoreboards — not two referees who might disagree.
5 · Screenshots are testimony; state is evidence

Here: twice a screenshot showed a UI state that turned out to be a stale mid-load frame; and one click "failed" only because it hit stale coordinates. Each time, reading the actual DOM (element.hidden, localStorage) or clicking via the DOM resolved it in one step.

Principle: when what-you-see contradicts what-should-be, check the underlying state before "fixing" code. Debugging the rendering of reality wastes hours; debugging reality is fast.

🧒 If the photo of the scoreboard looks wrong, walk over and read the scoreboard before accusing the scorekeeper.
6 · New environments flush out forgotten manual setup

Here: Pages deploys failed on the new repo though the identical workflow "worked" on the old one — because someone (past you!) had once enabled Pages by hand in the old repo's Settings. The workflow never had the power to do that itself.

Principle: every "works here, fails there" hides an undocumented one-time action. Pilots and clean-room clones are worth doing partly because they surface these.

🧒 The recipe "worked" at grandma's because her oven was already preheated. Cooking in a new kitchen reveals the missing first step.
7 · Deliberate omissions belong in the spec

Here: there is no "export the whole pool" button — on purpose: re-broadcasting someone else's bracket without their consent is exactly the leak this design exists to prevent. SPEC.md records it as a decision, so a future contributor doesn't "helpfully" add it.

Principle: the features you chose NOT to build are design decisions too. Undocumented, they read as gaps and get "fixed."

🧒 The playground has no gate on purpose — put a sign on the fence saying so, or someone will kindly install a gate.

If you remember one thing

Constraints did the designing. "Free" eliminated every backend. "Nobody surfaced without opting in" eliminated every registry. What survived both filters — a link one person creates and another person accepts — wasn't a compromise; it was the best version of the feature. Good constraints aren't the enemy of ambitious builds; they're the search algorithm.
